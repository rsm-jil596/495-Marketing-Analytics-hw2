Does Price Matter in Charitable Giving? Evidence from a Large-Scale Natural Field Experiment

Introduction

How much does price matter when people decide to donate to charity? This is the central question of Karlan and List (2007), one of the most influential papers in the economics of charitable giving. The authors ask whether offering a matching grant — where a lead donor promises to match every dollar raised — increases donations, and whether larger match ratios (2:1 or 3:1) are more effective than a smaller one (1:1).

To answer this, Karlan and List partnered with a US liberal nonprofit and ran a natural field experiment. In August 2005, they sent direct mail solicitations to 50,083 prior donors. Donors were randomly assigned to either a control group (standard letter) or a treatment group (letter announcing a matching grant). Within the treatment group, three dimensions were further randomized: the match ratio ($1:$1, $2:$1, or $3:$1), the maximum size of the matching grant ($25,000, $50,000, $100,000, or unstated), and the example donation amount suggested in the letter (low, medium, or high).

The paper’s main findings are:

  1. The match offer works. Announcing a match raises the response rate by about 22% and revenue per letter by 19%.
  2. Bigger ratios don’t help. A 2:1 or 3:1 match is no more effective than 1:1 — directly contradicting conventional fundraising wisdom.
  3. Political environment matters. The match effect is driven entirely by donors in red states (Bush 2004). In blue states, the match had no effect.

Main Analysis

Table 1: Balance Check

Before examining treatment effects, we need to confirm that random assignment produced comparable groups. If randomization worked, treatment and control donors should look statistically identical on pre-experiment characteristics — all p-values should be above 0.05.

Code 
balance_vars = {
    'MRM2':   'Months since last donation',
    'HPA':    'Highest previous contribution ($)',
    'freq':   'Number of prior donations',
    'years':  'Years since first donation',
    'dormant':'Had not yet given in 2005',
    'female': 'Female',
    'couple': 'Couple',
    'red0':   'Lives in red state',
    'redcty': 'Lives in red county',
}

rows = []
for var, label in balance_vars.items():
    t_v  = df.loc[df['treatment']==1, var].dropna().astype(float)
    c_v  = df.loc[df['control']==1,   var].dropna().astype(float)
    _, p = stats.ttest_ind(t_v, c_v)
    rows.append({
        'Variable':  label,
        'All':       f"{df[var].mean():.3f} ({df[var].std():.3f})",
        'Treatment': f"{t_v.mean():.3f} ({t_v.std():.3f})",
        'Control':   f"{c_v.mean():.3f} ({c_v.std():.3f})",
        'p-value':   f"{p:.3f}"
    })

pd.DataFrame(rows).style.hide(axis='index')

All p-values are well above 0.05, confirming that the two groups are statistically indistinguishable before the experiment. This validates the experimental design — any differences in giving behavior we observe can be causally attributed to the match offer.

The match offer raises the response rate from 1.8% (control) to 2.2% (any treatment) — a statistically significant 22% increase. Table 3: Regression Results

We estimate linear probability models (LPMs) regressing the binary donation outcome on treatment status.

Note on the “probit” label: The paper labels Table 3 as a probit regression, but the coefficients match OLS/LPM estimates, not probit marginal effects. This is a known quirk flagged in the assignment. We verify it below.

Code 
ols1 = smf.ols('gave ~ treatment', data=df).fit()
ols2 = smf.ols(
    'gave ~ treatment + ratio2 + ratio3 + size25 + size50 + size100 + askd2 + askd3',
    data=df
).fit()

var_labels = {
    'treatment': 'Treatment (any match)',
    'ratio2':    'Treatment × 2:1 ratio',
    'ratio3':    'Treatment × 3:1 ratio',
    'size25':    'Treatment × $25k threshold',
    'size50':    'Treatment × $50k threshold',
    'size100':   'Treatment × $100k threshold',
    'askd2':     'Treatment × medium ask',
    'askd3':     'Treatment × high ask',
    'Intercept': 'Constant',
}

def fmt(coef, se, pval):
    stars = ('***' if pval<.01 else '**' if pval<.05 else '*' if pval<.10 else '')
    return f"{coef:.4f}{stars} ({se:.4f})"

rows3 = []
for v in ['treatment','ratio2','ratio3','size25','size50','size100','askd2','askd3','Intercept']:
    c1 = fmt(ols1.params[v], ols1.bse[v], ols1.pvalues[v]) if v in ols1.params.index else '—'
    c2 = fmt(ols2.params[v], ols2.bse[v], ols2.pvalues[v]) if v in ols2.params.index else '—'
    rows3.append({'Variable': var_labels.get(v, v), 'Col (1)': c1, 'Col (2)': c2})

rows3.append({'Variable': 'N',
              'Col (1)': f"{int(ols1.nobs):,}",
              'Col (2)': f"{int(ols2.nobs):,}"})

pd.DataFrame(rows3).style.hide(axis='index')

Column (1) shows that any match offer increases the probability of donating by 0.42 percentage points (p < 0.01), matching the paper’s reported 0.004***. Column (2) confirms that the ratio2 and ratio3 interaction terms are near-zero and insignificant — larger match ratios add nothing.

We can also confirm the probit quirk directly:

# Run probit and compare marginal effect to OLS coefficient
probit1 = smf.probit('gave ~ treatment', data=df).fit(disp=0)
me = probit1.get_margeff()

print(f"OLS coefficient on treatment:    {ols1.params.treatment:.4f}")
print(f"Probit marginal effect:          {me.margeff[0]:.4f}")
print(f"Paper reports:                   0.004")
print()
print("=> OLS matches the paper. Table 3 reports OLS coefficients despite the probit label.")

Something Additional

Does the Match Effect Depend on Political Environment?

The most surprising result in the paper is that the match effect is entirely driven by donors in red states (states that voted for Bush in 2004). In blue states, the match had no statistically significant impact at all.

This is counterintuitive: the charity is a liberal organization, so you might expect its blue-state supporters to respond most enthusiastically to a peer signal. The opposite is true. The authors speculate this reflects political minority identity — donors who are ideologically in the minority in their state may be more responsive to a credibility signal from a like-minded peer, making the match announcement particularly motivating for red-state donors.

df['T_red0'] = df['treatment'] * df['red0']
rh = smf.ols('gave ~ treatment + red0 + T_red0', data=df).fit()

rh_rows = []
for v, label in [('treatment', 'Treatment (blue-state effect)'),
                 ('red0',      'Red state (baseline difference)'),
                 ('T_red0',    'Treatment × Red state'),
                 ('Intercept', 'Constant')]:
    stars = ('***' if rh.pvalues[v]<.01 else '**' if rh.pvalues[v]<.05 else '')
    rh_rows.append({'Variable': label,
                    'Coefficient': f"{rh.params[v]:.4f}{stars}",
                    'Std. Error':  f"({rh.bse[v]:.4f})"})
rh_rows.append({'Variable': 'N', 'Coefficient': f"{int(rh.nobs):,}", 'Std. Error': ''})

pd.DataFrame(rh_rows).style.hide(axis='index')

The regression confirms the pattern. The Treatment coefficient — the blue-state effect — is +0.001 and not significant. The Treatment × Red state interaction is +0.008 and significant at the 1% level, matching the paper’s reported 0.009***.

The practical implication for nonprofits is important: the effectiveness of a matching grant may depend heavily on the political and social context of the donors being solicited, not just the mechanics of the match ratio itself.
